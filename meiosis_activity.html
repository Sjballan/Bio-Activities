Clinical Application ~10 min Groups of 3
Click Reveal after your group agrees →
Case A
A newborn presents with hypotonia, upslanting palpebral fissures, a single palmar crease, and a small head. Maternal age was 42. Chromosome analysis shows 47 chromosomes.
Trisomy 21 — Down Syndrome

Extra chromosome 21, typically from nondisjunction in Meiosis I of the egg. Risk increases with advanced maternal age. FISH or karyotype confirms.

Case B
A 16-year-old is referred for primary amenorrhea. She is short (4'8"), has a webbed neck, low posterior hairline, and widely spaced nipples. Ultrasound shows streak gonads.
Turner Syndrome — 45, X

Loss of one X chromosome, often paternal in origin. Most common cause: nondisjunction during spermatogenesis. Estrogen replacement and growth hormone typically recommended.

Case C
A 20-year-old male is evaluated for infertility. He is tall (6'3"), has small testes, gynecomastia, and reduced facial hair. Testosterone is low; FSH and LH are elevated.
Klinefelter Syndrome — 47, XXY

Extra X chromosome in a male (46+1). Arises from nondisjunction in either parent. Seminiferous tubule dysgenesis leads to azoospermia. One Barr body is present.

Case D
A 19-year-old male is incidentally found to have a 47,XYY karyotype during a workup for severe acne and tall stature (6'4"). He is fertile and otherwise healthy.
Jacob Syndrome — 47, XYY

Extra Y results from nondisjunction in Meiosis II of spermatogenesis (paternal only). Often undiagnosed. Normal fertility. Historical behavioral associations are not supported by current evidence.

Case E
A 25-year-old woman presents with mild learning difficulties and menstrual irregularity. Karyotype reveals 47,XXX. She has no dysmorphic features and is otherwise healthy.
Triple X Syndrome — 47, XXX

Extra X from maternal nondisjunction. Two Barr bodies present. Most females are fertile; phenotype is highly variable and often mild. Occurs in ~1/1000 females.

05

Big Picture Discussion 💬

No wrong answers — think critically and discuss with your group.

Critical Thinking ~8 min Open discussion
Prompts — pick at least two, jot your key points below
  1. Why does meiosis, not mitosis, generate chromosomal aneuploidies in gametes? What specific safeguards in meiosis can fail?
  2. Jacob syndrome (47, XYY) is often called "benign." What ethical issues arise when we label an aneuploidy as benign vs. severe? Who benefits from prenatal testing in this case?
  3. Down syndrome is the most common viable trisomy. Why do you think trisomy 21 is survivable when most other autosomal trisomies are lethal? What is special about chromosome 21?
  4. Triple X syndrome often goes undiagnosed for life. Should it be included in routine newborn screening panels? Argue for or against.
  5. Maternal age is a well-known risk factor for trisomy 21. If you were a genetic counselor, how would you explain this risk to a 38-year-old patient considering pregnancy? What information would you prioritize?
